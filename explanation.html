1 · Project overview

A movie platform receives ~300,000 reviews a month. Buried inside are customers saying "I was charged twice, I'm leaving." Nobody can read them all, so those customers churn unnoticed.

review-radar reads every review the moment it arrives and answers two different questions:

  1. Is this negative? — answered by a machine learning model trained on 25,000 labeled examples.
  2. Is this urgent? — answered by a transparent rule engine, because escalation must be explainable.

The two answers combine into an action: urgent complaints go to a human immediately, calm criticism goes to the product backlog, positive reviews go to analytics.

2 · Problem

Formal ML statement

TaskBinary text classification — positive vs negative
InputRaw review text (unstructured, variable length)
OutputP(negative) ∈ [0,1] — a probability, not a hard label
HorizonReal-time, per review event
Quality gateF1(negative) ≥ 0.85
Latency budget< 100 ms (achieved: ~2 ms)
Why a probability and not yes/no? Because the business decides where to draw the line — and that line is set by money (§10). A hard yes/no throws away the information needed to make that choice.

Why machine learning is required

ApproachWhy it fails
Humans read every review300k × 30s ≈ 2,500 hours/month ≈ 15 FTE — and still days behind
Keyword blacklistfires on "not bad at all!"; misses negation, sarcasm, unseen phrasing; endless manual upkeep
Learn from labeled data✅ derives patterns from 25,000 examples, including ones no human would think to list

3 · Business need

Two goals, in priority order:

  1. Retention — reply to at-risk customers within hours, not weeks. This is where the money is.
  2. Cost — stop paying staff to read reviews by hand.

The economic model

AssumptionValueMeaning
Reviews / month300,000platform volume
Negative share20%≈ 60,000 negative reviews
At-risk share p30%angry reviewers who would actually leave
Win-back rate r15%at-risk customers saved by outreach
ARPU × months M$10 × 6 = $60value of a retained customer
Contact cost C$1cost of one outreach
Read these honestly. This is a model of the business, not a bank statement — it rests on assumed win-back and at-risk rates. The defensible claim isn't the headline dollar figure; it's that break-even precision is 11% while we operate at 87%. An 8× margin means the program profits even if these assumptions are badly wrong (§10).

4 · Dataset src/data.py

IMDB Large Movie Review Dataset — Maas et al., 2011 (Stanford). The field's standard sentiment benchmark.

Size50,000 labeled reviews
Split25,000 train / 25,000 test
Balanceexactly 50/50 positive/negative
Label encoding0 = negative, 1 = positive
Label qualityonly strong opinions kept (≥7★ or ≤4★) → clean, unambiguous
from datasets import load_dataset

def load_imdb(cache_dir=DATA_DIR):
    dataset = load_dataset("stanfordnlp/imdb", cache_dir=str(cache_dir))
    return dataset["train"].to_pandas(), dataset["test"].to_pandas()
Why the train/test split is sacred: the model never sees the 25k test reviews during training. Grading on studied data is an exam with the answers leaked. Every number in this documentation comes from unseen data.
Honest limitation — say this before you're asked. IMDB is a stand-in. It's movie reviews, balanced 50/50, and contains no billing complaints. Real platform reviews are ~80% positive and full of "charged twice" language the model never saw. This is precisely why the urgency engine is rule-based (§11) and why monthly retraining on our own reviews is in the roadmap (§16).

6 · Feature engineering src/train.py — TfidfVectorizer

Computers can't read words, only numbers. TF-IDF converts each review into a vector of 50,000 numbers.

In
"This Movie Was a TOTAL waste of time!!!"
→
Out
the · movie · was → ≈ 0.00
waste → 0.61  ·  total → 0.28
ComponentMeaningEffect
TF — term frequencyhow often a word appears in this reviewrepeated words weigh more
IDF — inverse document frequencyhow rare the word is across all reviewsfiller words are crushed to ~0
sublinear_tf=Trueuses 1 + log(count) instead of raw count10× "bad" isn't 10× as negative
ngram_range=(1,2)keeps word pairs toocaptures negation — see below
L2 normalizationscales each vector to unit lengthlong reviews don't dominate short ones

Bigrams earn their place — with evidence

Single words can't tell "good" from "not good". Word pairs can. This isn't theory — two of the model's strongest learned features are pairs:

"the worst" −5.74 "the best" +3.89

And the experiment sweep (§12) quantifies it: adding bigrams was worth +0.011 F1 — the single biggest improvement of any change tested.

The elegance: nobody wrote a list of "important words." TF-IDF discovers which words carry opinion purely from statistics. That is what "feature engineering" means here.

7 · Training pipeline src/train.py

The whole model is a two-step scikit-learn Pipeline — vectorizer then classifier, treated as one object so the exact same transformation is applied at train and at inference time.

def build_pipeline(max_features=50_000, C=1.0):
    return Pipeline([
        ("tfidf", TfidfVectorizer(max_features=max_features,
                                  ngram_range=(1, 2),
                                  sublinear_tf=True)),
        ("clf", LogisticRegression(C=C, max_iter=1000)),
    ])
Why a Pipeline and not two separate steps? It eliminates train/serve skew — the #1 source of silent production ML bugs. The vectorizer's learned vocabulary and IDF values travel with the classifier inside one pickle. Impossible to accidentally apply different preprocessing at inference.

How Logistic Regression works

It learns one weight per feature. To score a review it multiplies each present feature by its weight, sums, and squashes the total into a probability:

z = intercept + Σ (tfidf[word] × weight[word])
P(negative) = 1 − sigmoid(z)

8 · Model selection

The questions an examiner will ask — answered with reasons, not preferences.

CandidateReasoningVerdict
Logistic RegressionLinear models dominate high-dimensional sparse text. Convex objective → one global optimum, no seed lottery. Outputs well-calibrated probabilities — non-negotiable, since §10 optimizes a threshold over them. Weights are directly readable.SHIPPED
Random ForestTrees split one feature at a time; across 50,000 sparse dimensions with thinly-spread signal that's structurally a bad fit. Slower, far larger on disk, and probabilities are poorly calibrated out of the box.rejected
Linear SVMGenuinely competitive on accuracy — the closest call. But it outputs a decision margin, not a probability; recovering one needs Platt scaling — an extra calibration stage for no measured gain.rejected
Naive BayesFast and a classic text baseline, but its feature-independence assumption is badly violated by bigrams, and it's known to produce overconfident probabilities — again fatal for threshold work.rejected
DistilBERT (pretrained)Reads context, handles negation natively. We benchmarked it properly — it tied the baseline (0.898 vs 0.900) at ~100× the inference cost.benchmarked
Fine-tuned BERTWould likely reach ~0.93. Requires a GPU and hours of training to buy +3 points we don't need — the gate is 0.85 and we're at 0.90.out of scope
The industrial argument: accuracy stopped being the binding constraint the moment we cleared the gate. The real constraints are latency, inference cost, and explainability — and on all three the simple model wins outright. Reaching for the biggest available model is a student reflex; proving the cheap one suffices is an engineering decision.

9 · Evaluation src/evaluate.py

All metrics target the negative class. Accuracy is deliberately not used.

Why accuracy is a trap: in production ~80% of reviews are positive. A model that answers "positive" to everything scores 80% accuracy and catches zero angry customers. Accuracy rewards the useless model.
MetricQuestion it answersScore
Recall (negative)Of all truly angry reviews, how many did we catch?0.896
Precision (negative)Of those we flagged, how many really were angry?0.904
F1 (negative)Harmonic mean of the two — the project gate0.900
Precision@100Of the 100 we're most confident about, how many are right?1.00
Precision@1000Of the top 1,000, how many are right?1.00
Precision@k is the real operational KPI. Support has finite hours and works the queue top-down. precision@1000 = 1.00 means the first thousand tickets are all genuinely negative — zero wasted agent time exactly where capacity is scarcest.

Calibration — the quiet prerequisite

Notebook 02 plots a calibration curve. This matters more than it looks: §10 chooses a threshold by comparing probabilities to a cost ratio. If the model said "0.8" for things that are negative only 50% of the time, the entire profit calculation would be built on sand. Logistic regression is naturally well-calibrated — one more reason it was chosen over Naive Bayes or an SVM.

10 · Threshold optimization src/threshold.py · notebooks/04

Review feedback #2: "optimize the threshold on cost and expected return, not only model quality."

What a threshold is

The model outputs P(negative) ∈ [0,1]. The threshold is the line where we act. 0.5 is a convention, not a law — it's simply where two classes are equally likely, which has nothing to do with what they cost.

The economics

Contacting one flagged customer costs C = $1.

Expected value of one contact that finds an at-risk customer: r × ARPU × M = 0.15 × $10 × 6 = $9.

break-even precision = C / (r·ARPU·M) = $1 / $9 ≈ 0.111

Because a save is worth 9× what a contact costs, outreach profits as long as at least 11% of contacted customers were genuinely at risk. That's a very low bar — the correct response is to cast a wider net, i.e. lower the threshold.

The implementation

for t in thresholds:                       # sweep 0.01 → 0.99
    flagged   = p_negative >= t
    contacted = flagged.sum() * scale     # scale test set → monthly volume
    caught    = (flagged & is_negative).sum() * scale
    saved     = caught * p_at_risk * win_back_rate
    profit    = saved * value_per_save − contacted * contact_cost

Results

QuantityValueInterpretation
Break-even precision0.111the profit floor
Default threshold0.50the arbitrary convention
Profit-optimal threshold0.44below 0.5 — as the theory predicts
Precision at optimum0.8718× above break-even → low-risk
Contacted / month159,948the wide net
Profit at optimum~$216k/movs $214k at t=0.5
The finding: model-quality metrics and business-value metrics select different thresholds, and the business one wins. The threshold is not a hyperparameter — it's a business lever, and it now has a function that computes it.
Intellectual honesty (say this out loud). On this model the gain over t=0.5 is small — about $1.9k/month — because the model is well-calibrated and confident, so it rarely emits mid-range scores where the threshold would bite. The gap widens sharply with a noisier model or realistic 80/20 class imbalance. The reusable method is the deliverable, not one lucky number. When C, r, or ARPU change, re-run the sweep and the threshold updates itself.

11 · Urgency engine src/urgency.py

Review feedback #1: "how does the system distinguish negative from truly urgent? Many negative reviews are constructive and need no intervention."

Correct — and unaddressed in the original design. "The ending felt rushed" and "charged twice, cancelling NOW" are both negative, and treating them identically drowns the support team and buries the real fires.

Design decision: rules, not ML

A support lead must be able to see exactly why a ticket jumped the queue — and a customer must be able to appeal it. Our engine returns the literal matched words. A black box asserting "trust me, it's urgent" is operationally unusable. Additionally, we have no labeled urgency data — IMDB has sentiment labels, not urgency labels. Inventing them would be fabrication. Rules are the honest choice here.

Signal groups and weights

GroupExamplesWeightRationale
Churn intentcancel · refund · never again · switching to0.45explicit statement of leaving — the strongest possible signal
Moneycharged · billing · overcharged · took my money0.30billing errors churn customers fastest
Angerscam · fraud · unacceptable · disgusting0.15emotional intensity, but alone it's just venting
Shouting!!! density · ALL-CAPS ratio0.10weak signal — supporting evidence only

The routing matrix — the answer to "who needs intervention?"

SentimentUrgencyRouteWhy
Negative≥ 0.4support_urgentcustomer is leaving — act within hours
Negative< 0.4feedback_backlogvaluable criticism — review weekly
Positive—analyticstrack trends, no action

Verified behavior

ReviewP(neg)UrgencyRoute
"Absolutely terrible... charged twice... I want a REFUND, cancelling today!!!"0.8040.989support_urgent
"The pacing dragged and the ending felt rushed, though the cinematography was decent."0.6180.000feedback_backlog

Note row 2 — this is the feedback being answered: a genuinely negative review (0.618) that correctly does not page a human.

Known limitation. The lexicon is English and hand-curated. It will miss creative phrasing and could be gamed. The upgrade path once real labeled data exists: train a supervised urgency classifier and keep the rules as an explainable fallback. Documented, not hidden.

What is and isn't in git

CommittedIgnored (rebuilt on demand)
all source, tests, notebooks, docsdata/ — 130MB, downloads in seconds
requirements.txt — pinned floorsmodels/*.pkl — rebuilt by src.train
.gitignoremlflow.db, mlruns/ — local experiment history
Why not commit the model? Binary artifacts bloat git history forever and can't be diffed. The recipe is committed; the output is reproducible in one command. That's the standard industry split — and MLflow is what makes it safe, since every artifact is tracked with the parameters that produced it.
